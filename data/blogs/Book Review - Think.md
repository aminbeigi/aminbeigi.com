---
title: Book Review - Think
date: 2026-02-22
---

Think by Simon Blackburn is designed to be an introduction to philosophy. In under 300 pages, it covers the major domains: epistemology, ethics, metaphysics and etc. The breadth of topics is vast but depth/detail quite thin.

Blackburn is clear about the goal of the book. You're not reading this to become a philosophy wizard but instead train your thinking skills. He says:

> Success will not be a matter of how much you know at the end, but of what you can do when the going gets tough.

This makes sense. The book is less about absorbing too much and more about strengthening your ability to reason when dealing intellectual pressure. Pressure you feel when you're ideas are challenged and you're forced to think more critically.

He is quite opinionated. Reading the book can feel like moving along guardrails. You're free to look around, but the direction of travel is strongly guided. For a beginner, that guidance is helpful, given how intimating philosophy can be. Still, I think there's a trade-off with the clarify of the path at the expense of interpretive autonomy.

For example, he treats the idea of the universe as a "necessary being" with a level of confidence that many philosophers would consider controversial. It's far from universally accepted, yet the presentation can make it feel close to settled. Like we should take it as gospel.

A warning: the book is entirely theoretical philosophy rather than practical philosophy. It's nothing like (for example) Meditations by Marcus Aurelius, which is explicitly practical and aimed at shaping how you live. Think is about improving the scaffolding of your thought structure, not directly how you act.

Overall, Think can feel intense - moving quickly from one mind-bending concept to another. It works best read slowly, in sections, letting the ideas settle. You won't walk away with final answers, but you will walk away sharper.
